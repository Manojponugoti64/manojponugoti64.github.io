HOW TO BUILD THE FUTURE

June 2, 2026 · Interview with Jack Altman, 2016

In 2016, before the world knew what GPT stood for, before OpenAI became a household name, before any of this — Jack Altman sat down with his brother Sam and asked him the simplest question anyone can ask a builder:

How do you decide what to work on?

The answer Sam gives in that twenty-minute conversation is, I think, one of the most honest pieces of career advice ever recorded. Not because it's new. Because it's true in the way that things you already know but have never heard said out loud are true.

Here's what stayed with me.

The Intersection

Most people fall into their work. They don't choose it. They stumble into a job, then another, then another, and one day they look up and realize the trajectory was never theirs — it was just gravity.

Sam says the antidote is deceptively simple. Find the intersection of three things:

What you're good at. What you enjoy. What creates value for the world.

Three circles. One overlap. That's it.

But here's what most people miss: two out of three isn't enough. You can be brilliant at something you hate — and you'll burn out. You can love something the world doesn't need — and you'll starve. You can create enormous value doing work that slowly hollows you out — and you'll wake up at forty wondering where you went.

The intersection is small. Finding it takes time. But ignoring it costs more.

The Wrong Kind of Risk

There's a line in this interview that I think about more than almost anything else I've read about building things:

"The wrong kind of risk is where people don't actually do things or don't commit to something because they don't want to fail and they overrate the risk of failure and the reputation damage or embarrassment or whatever."

Read that carefully. He's not talking about recklessness. He's not saying jump off a cliff without a plan.

He's saying the most dangerous thing you can do is nothing.

The risk everyone calculates — the risk of failing publicly, of looking stupid, of building something nobody wants — that risk is real but it's recoverable. You fail. You learn. You build again. The world forgets faster than you think.

The risk nobody calculates is the one that actually kills you: the risk of never starting. Of spending years as a talker instead of a doer. Of reaching the end with a clean record and an empty portfolio.

"I think one really important thing to strive for in your career is to be a doer, not a talker."

Six words that separate people who build from people who plan to build.

Compounding in Your Twenties

Sam says something about your twenties that sounds almost brutal in its simplicity: work harder than you think you should.

Not because suffering is noble. Not because hustle culture is right. But because of compounding.

The skills you build at twenty-three compound for forty years. The relationships you form when you have nothing to offer except effort and genuine interest — those compound too. The reputation you build as someone who shows up, who ships, who does the unglamorous work without being asked — that compounds most of all.

Your twenties are when the interest rate is highest and the principal is cheapest. Every hour invested now returns tenfold later. Not because the universe is fair. Because mathematics doesn't care about fairness — it only cares about time.

And you have more of it now than you ever will again.

Finding Your Tribe

There are pockets of people around the world doing extraordinary work. Small clusters. Usually quiet. Usually not on social media performing their ambition for an audience.

Sam's advice: find them. Move toward them. Position yourself near the gravity of people who are building things that matter.

Not to network. Networking is transactional, and transactional relationships have a half-life measured in months.

Instead: help people. Help them with no expectation of return. No mental ledger. No "I did this so they'll do that." Just help, because the kind of person who helps without keeping score is the kind of person other builders want to keep close.

Sam says this has benefited him enormously over his career — but always on a delay. The help you give in 2024 pays off in 2027, in ways you can't predict, from directions you didn't expect. You can't engineer it. You can only earn it by being genuine long enough that the compounding kicks in.

When to Stop

This is the question nobody has a clean answer to. When do you quit?

Sam doesn't pretend to have a formula. But he offers a principle that I think is as close as you can get:

Stop when you've genuinely run out of ideas.

Not when it's hard. It's always hard. Not when people don't get it — people never get it at first. Not when you've been at it for two months and nothing is working — two months is nothing.

Stop when you've exhausted every angle. When you've tried everything you can think of and you're staring at a wall with no doors. When the well isn't just dry — it's gone.

Most people quit too early. They try something for a few weeks, it doesn't immediately work, and they pivot. Then they try something else for a few weeks. Then something else. They mistake movement for progress and end up with a resume full of abandoned projects and no scar tissue.

The builders who win are the ones who stay long enough to find the door that everyone else walked past.

The Engine Inside

Motivation sourced from other people is borrowed fuel. It burns fast and leaves nothing.

"The most successful people I know are primarily internally driven. They do what they do to impress themselves and because they feel compelled to make something happen in the world."

Not to beat a competitor. Not to prove a doubter wrong. Not to perform success for an audience of followers.

They build because they can't not build. Because the idea lives in their head and the only way to get relief is to make it real. Because the gap between what exists and what should exist is physically uncomfortable to them, and building is the only medicine.

That's not discipline. That's compulsion dressed in a work ethic.

And it's the only kind of motivation that survives the long middle — the months and years between the excitement of starting and the satisfaction of finishing, where most people quietly give up and tell themselves they were being "strategic."

Almost Delusional Self-Belief

Sam uses a word that would make any rational person uncomfortable: delusional.

Not clinically. Not dangerously. But he suggests that to build anything that matters, you need a level of self-belief that, from the outside, looks almost unreasonable. You need to believe your work matters when no one is using it. You need to believe the vision is right when every metric says it's wrong. You need to hold the thread when the world is pulling it out of your hands.

This isn't arrogance. Arrogance is loud and brittle. This is something quieter and much harder — a deep, private certainty that what you're doing will work, even though you can't prove it yet. Even though you might be wrong.

The difference between a founder who makes it and one who doesn't is often just this: the willingness to keep believing six months longer than is comfortable.

What I Took Away

This interview is ten years old. Sam Altman has since built one of the most consequential companies in human history. The world has changed in ways that make 2016 feel like a different century.

But the advice hasn't aged a day.

Find the intersection. Take the right risks. Be a doer. Work harder than feels reasonable while you're young enough that the compounding has time to work. Find your tribe and help them without keeping score. Don't quit until you've genuinely exhausted every idea. And fuel the whole thing with an internal engine that doesn't need applause to keep running.

None of this is complicated. All of it is hard.

And that's exactly why it works.
