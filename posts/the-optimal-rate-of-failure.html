THE OPTIMAL RATE OF FAILURE

April 28, 2026

The economist George Stigler once said, "If you never miss a plane, you're spending too much time at the airport." It's a small line with a big idea tucked inside it. If you never fail at anything, you're probably not being careful -you're being too cautious. And caution, it turns out, has a price.

We tend to celebrate people who don't fail. We promote them, follow them, hand them the microphone. But quiet, perfect track records often hide something less flattering than skill: the refusal to ever bet on something uncertain. The people who never miss a plane are also the people who spend their lives in airport lounges.

For almost anything worth doing, the right rate of failure is not zero. Sometimes it's nowhere close to zero.

Why a Little Failure is a Sign of Health Imagine a friend who is learning to play the guitar. In their first year, every chord is clean, every song is recognizable, and they never hit a wrong note. You wouldn't think, what a prodigy. You'd think, they're only practicing the three songs they already know. A learning musician who never sounds bad isn't learning -they're hiding.

The same logic applies almost everywhere. A scientist whose experiments always confirm the hypothesis is probably asking timid questions. A chef whose dishes always work out is probably cooking the same five recipes. A writer whose drafts are always good is probably writing the same essay over and over.

Failure, in moderation, is the receipt you get for trying something that wasn't guaranteed. No receipt, no risk. No risk, usually no growth.

The Math Behind the Idea Whenever we try something, the expected value of the attempt is the sum of three things:

What we gain if it works - the direct benefit, plus the doors it opens for the future. What we lose if it fails - the damage, the wasted resources, the missed alternatives. What it costs us just to try -time, money, energy, attention. There's also a fourth, sneakier ingredient: information. Even a failed attempt teaches us something. Sometimes that lesson is worth more than the attempt itself.

The "optimal rate of failure" is just the failure rate you'd expect if you were taking exactly the right amount of risk given those costs and rewards.

If trying is cheap, failure is mild, and success is huge - the optimal failure rate is high. You should try a lot. If trying is expensive, failure is catastrophic, and success is only a small win - the optimal failure rate is low. You should try carefully and rarely. So when I tell a friend, "I think this idea has a 90% chance of failing - you should absolutely do it," I'm not being reckless. I'm doing the math. If the cost of trying is small and the upside is enormous, a 90% failure rate can still be the smartest bet on the table.

Failure Is Not Always Bad News, and Success Is Not Always Good News Here's the part that takes a moment to sit with: if the optimal rate of failure for a path is high, then seeing a lot of failure along that path is not a problem. It's a sign that the right experiments are being run.

We already accept this in some places. We don't expect a beginner musician to sound polished. We don't expect a startup's first product to be its last. We don't expect a scientist's first hypothesis to be the right one.

But we forget the flip side, which is just as important: if you never see someone fail, something might be quietly wrong.

A musician who never plays badly might just never be playing at all. A founder with a flawless résumé might be choosing only safe bets that don't matter. A team that ships only successes might be shipping only the obvious. The absence of failure isn't always a sign of mastery. Sometimes it's a sign of avoidance.

A Counterintuitive Example: The "Perfectly Safe" Office Picture two software teams.

Team A ships ten features a quarter. Two of them flop badly. The team writes honest postmortems, kills what isn't working, and doubles down on what is.

Team B ships three features a quarter. Every single one works. No bugs, no rollbacks, no embarrassing meetings.

On paper, Team B looks like the model of competence. But pay attention to what they're not doing. They're not trying anything they're unsure of. They're not exploring. They're not learning anything they didn't already know. They look perfect because they've quietly stopped taking the kinds of bets that would tell them something new.

Team A is failing more - and learning more, building more, and probably, over a few years, winning more.

A workplace with no visible failure isn't necessarily a healthy workplace. It might be a workplace where nobody dares to try.

"But Doesn't This Justify Anything?" A fair worry: can't anyone defend a bad decision after the fact by saying, "Well, the optimal rate of failure was high"? Yes - and that's why this idea has to be used honestly.

Three habits keep it grounded:

Predict before you act. Decide in advance what kind of failure rate is acceptable, and why. Argue the case ahead of time. Be able to explain why a risky plan is worth it before you know how it turned out. Check yourself afterwards. Compare the failure rate you predicted with the one you got. Calibration matters. This isn't a shield against criticism. It's a discipline for thinking clearly about uncertainty.

The Quiet Inequality of Risk There's one more piece of this idea that doesn't get talked about enough.

The "right" amount of risk for a person depends heavily on what failure would cost that specific person. If failing means a bruised ego and a few months of lost time, you can afford to swing big. If failing means losing your home, your visa, your family's stability- you can't.

Which means the people most free to take ambitious, world-improving risks are usually the people who already have a cushion. And the people who could often do the most good -talented, motivated, but without a safety net ,are often forced into the safer path, not because they want to be cautious, but because they cannot afford not to be.

Looked at this way, creating safety nets for ambitious people isn't just kindness. It's leverage. Every form of insurance, fellowship, stipend, or runway that lowers the personal cost of failure unlocks bolder, more original work from people who otherwise would have had to play it safe.

If we want more good ideas in the world, we don't just need more brave people. We need fewer reasons for brave people to be afraid.

So, How Often Should You Be Failing? Probably more than you currently are.

Not catastrophically. Not recklessly. But often enough that, when you look back on the last year, there are a few attempts you can point to and honestly say: that didn't work.

If you can't think of any, the question isn't whether you're being successful.

The question is whether you're really trying, Try more - Fail more.
