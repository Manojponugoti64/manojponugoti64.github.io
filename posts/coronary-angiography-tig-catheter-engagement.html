Coronary Angiography: Engaging the Ostia with the TIG Catheter

July 3, 2026 · A learner's walkthrough

Study aid, not a substitute for training. This is an educational walkthrough written for a cardiology trainee learning the mental model behind diagnostic coronary angiography. Real catheter skill is acquired hands-on, under direct supervision, on real hardware and fluoroscopy. Always follow your cath-lab's protocols, your attending's guidance, and current society guidelines. Nothing here replaces proctored bedside teaching.

When you start in the cath lab, three questions come up again and again: what exactly is the ostium, where does it sit, and how do I get a catheter to sit in it cleanly? This post builds that picture from the anatomy outward, then walks through engaging the left and right coronary arteries with the TIG (Tiger) catheter — the single "universal" curve most people learn first on the radial approach.

1. What is the ostium?

The coronary ostium is simply the mouth (the opening) where a coronary artery originates from the aorta. There are normally two of them, and they do not come off the aorta randomly — they arise from small out-pouchings just above the aortic valve called the sinuses of Valsalva (the aortic sinuses).

  • Left coronary ostium — arises from the left coronary sinus. It leads into the left main (LM), which quickly divides into the LAD and the LCx. It usually sits a little higher and more posterior.
  • Right coronary ostium — arises from the right coronary sinus. It leads into the RCA. It usually sits a little lower and more anterior.
  • The third sinus — the non-coronary sinus — has no coronary and points posteriorly, toward the interatrial septum.

Because the two ostia sit in different sinuses at slightly different heights and orientations, a catheter shaped to reach one is not automatically pointed at the other. That single fact is the reason engagement is a skill: you are steering the catheter tip into a specific mouth on a moving, pulsatile root.

2. The TIG (Tiger) catheter, and why it is "universal"

Historically, diagnostic angiography used two shapes: a Judkins Left (JL) for the left coronary and a Judkins Right (JR) for the right. Each is pre-shaped so that, once seated in the aorta, it naturally drops into its target ostium.

The TIG / Tiger catheter (a Terumo design popularised for transradial access) is a single catheter engineered to reach both coronaries. That is why it is called a "universal" catheter — one catheter, one puncture, both arteries, less catheter exchange. Its distinctive tip has a short, sharply angled secondary curve that can be pointed up-and-over for the left main, then re-oriented downward for the RCA, mostly by rotation rather than by swapping catheters.

3. Before you engage: the ground rules

These habits are what separate a clean, safe engagement from a dangerous one:

  • Always advance over a wire. The catheter travels from the radial (or femoral) artery up to the aortic root on a 0.035" J-tipped guidewire. The wire leads; the catheter follows. Never push a bare catheter tip up the arch.
  • Park in the aortic root / above the valve, then remove the wire. Position the tip in the ascending aorta pointing toward the aortic valve before you start hunting for the ostium.
  • Flush and connect to pressure. Aspirate, flush, and watch the arterial pressure waveform continuously. A crisp waveform means the tip is free in the lumen.
  • Watch for damping / ventricularization. If the pressure damps (flattens) or ventricularizes when you engage, the tip may be against the wall, deep, or in a small/diseased ostium (e.g. the conus branch, or a left main with disease). Do not inject forcefully. Disengage, reassess.
  • Small test puff first. A gentle contrast puff confirms coaxial, free-flowing position before a full injection.
  • Move gently and in small increments. The root is pulsatile; engagement is millimetres of push/pull and small turns of the wrist, not big shoves.

Mental model

Engagement = height (push/pull to set the tip at the level of the sinus) + rotation (turn the tip toward the correct sinus) + seating (the tip drops into the mouth and the pressure stays crisp). With the TIG you mostly change one variable at a time.

4. Engaging the LEFT coronary (left main) with the TIG

The left main ostium is the easier of the two to find with a universal catheter, because the TIG's hook is built to point up-and-leftward into the left sinus. A typical radial sequence:

  1. Advance the TIG over the J-wire into the ascending aorta, tip near the aortic valve plane. Remove the wire; aspirate and flush; connect pressure.
  2. Let the tip sit just above the valve. The hook naturally tends to fall toward the left coronary sinus.
  3. Apply gentle counter-clockwise rotation (in most radial setups) with slight advancement/withdrawal to point the tip posterolaterally toward the left ostium. Small adjustments — watch the tip on fluoroscopy.
  4. When the tip drops into the ostium you will often feel it "settle." Confirm with a test puff and a crisp pressure waveform.
  5. Image the LCA in your standard projections (see the view table below).

5. Engaging the RIGHT coronary (RCA) with the TIG

The RCA is where the "universal" catheter earns its name — and where beginners struggle most, because you re-purpose the same tip for a lower, anterior ostium. The key move is clockwise rotation to swing the tip toward the right sinus.

  1. Starting from the aortic root (often after imaging the left system, without exchanging catheters), position the tip near the right sinus, slightly above the valve.
  2. Apply slow clockwise rotation (typically ~90–180°) so the tip sweeps anteriorly toward the right coronary sinus. Rotate in small steps and let the catheter "catch up" — torque can lag, then whip.
  3. Often a small withdrawal as you rotate helps the tip dip down and "hook" the RCA ostium, which sits lower and points more anteriorly.
  4. Feel/see the tip settle into the ostium. Confirm with a test puff and a crisp, non-damped waveform. A damped RCA pressure can mean ostial disease, a conus-branch cannulation, or a deep/selective seating — back off before injecting.
  5. Image the RCA in your standard projections.

Common RCA pitfalls

Conus branch injection (tip too high/anterior → damping and the risk of provoking VF with a forceful injection), catheter dive into the RV branch, and over-torque whip where stored rotation releases suddenly and the tip jumps past the ostium. Rotate slowly, watch pressure, inject gently.

6. Which pictures to take (standard projections)

Engaging is only half the job — you then image each vessel in several angles so overlapping segments are separated and foreshortening is minimised. A common starting set (your lab will have its own house standard):

VesselUseful projectionsWhat it shows well
Left (LM/LAD/LCx) Spider (LAO caudal), LAO cranial, RAO caudal, RAO cranial, AP cranial LM bifurcation & ostial LAD/LCx (spider); mid/distal LAD & diagonals (cranial views); proximal LAD & LCx/OMs (RAO caudal)
Right (RCA) LAO (straight), RAO, and a cranial view (e.g. AP/LAO cranial) for the crux/PDA Proximal-to-mid RCA (LAO), mid RCA (RAO), and the distal bifurcation into PDA/PLV (cranial)

Quick angle intuition

LAO vs RAO = which side of the patient the image intensifier is on (left vs right anterior oblique). Cranial vs caudal = whether it is angled toward the head or the feet. Combine them to "open up" bifurcations and lay out segments that would otherwise overlap.

7. Putting it together

  • The ostium is the coronary's mouth on the aorta, arising from the left and right sinuses of Valsalva.
  • The TIG/Tiger is a universal radial catheter: one shape for both coronaries, minimising exchanges.
  • Left main: sit above the valve, gentle counter-clockwise rotation, let the hook settle into the left sinus.
  • RCA: slow clockwise rotation with a small withdrawal to hook the lower, anterior right sinus.
  • Throughout: advance over a wire, watch the pressure waveform, test-puff before you inject, and move in millimetres.

Read this to build the map in your head — then get proctored time on the table. The feel of torque lag, the "settle" of a seated tip, and the instant recognition of a damped waveform are things your hands learn, not your eyes. Ask your attending to talk you through their rotation cues on the next diagnostic case; the muscle memory comes fast once the anatomy is clear.
