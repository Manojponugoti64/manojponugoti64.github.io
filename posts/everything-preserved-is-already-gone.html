EVERYTHING PRESERVED IS ALREADY GONE

July 4, 2026 · A photo essay

The same butterfly, fourteen walls. Or fourteen butterflies, one wall — after a while it stops mattering which. A dead thing, wings flattened, held to a decaying surface by a small square of clear tape. That square is the whole story. Someone found it, and instead of letting it fall, they kept it.

Butterflies are the laziest symbol we have for transformation. But these ones aren’t transforming. They’re finished. And the walls behind them — the things we build to last — are the ones visibly dying: rust blooming through paint, paint peeling off in continents, posters weathering down to their bones. The fragile thing is preserved. The permanent thing is going.

One subject, fourteen palettes. Pink is tender. Blue is cold. Rust is violent. Yellow is feverish. The fact never changes — only the light you see it in. Which is, more or less, how grief works.

The tape square says: this was found, then kept. Not composed — collected. A field specimen with the label missing.

In some frames the wing patterns rhyme with the rust stains around them, and the insect almost dissolves into the wall — as if the surface were absorbing it. As if it had always been just another blemish.

One photo of a dead butterfly on a wall is a nice photo. Fourteen of them, same framing, is a statement. It stops being about the butterfly and becomes about looking — returning to the same small loss again and again until the looking itself is the point.

We tape things down because we can’t hold them. The wall keeps peeling anyway. The rust keeps spreading anyway. The square of tape holds exactly one thing still while everything around it moves.

Everything preserved is already gone. That’s not the sad part. The sad part would have been letting it fall without looking.
