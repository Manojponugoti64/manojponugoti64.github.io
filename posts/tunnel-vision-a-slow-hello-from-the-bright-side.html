TUNNEL VISION: A SLOW HELLO FROM THE BRIGHT SIDE

April 30, 2026

The phone rings the way phones used to ring, when ringing meant something — a small, patient sound that asks the world to pause.

Mira holds it against her ear with both hands. Her hands shake a little now, the way leaves shake in a wind that hasn’t fully arrived yet. On the other end, August picks up on the third ring.

“Hello?” he says, and his voice does the thing his voice always does, which is make a room feel smaller and warmer at the same time.

“Hello from the other side,” she says, because she has been waiting six years to use that line, and because the other side is, finally, a real place.

· · ·

The summer of the long Latin word

They met at a support group the summer she turned sixteen. The disease was a long Latin word that nobody at the door bothered to pronounce. It only meant one thing in plain English: slowly. Everything would happen slowly. Slowly, a lung would tire. Slowly, a stair would become a mountain. Slowly, slowly, slowly, until quickly, at the end, would simply be one more slow thing.

She remembers the chair he sat in at her first meeting, the way the light caught the unlit cigarette he never smoked. He told her, with a perfectly straight face, that he kept it between his teeth as a metaphor — you put the killing thing right between your teeth and you don’t give it the power to do its killing. She laughed for the first time in three months. He looked at her like she had said something brave.

“Okay?” he asked.

“Okay,” she said.

That word would survive everything else. They used it like a flashlight, like a vow, like a small portable home. Okay for the diagnosis. Okay for the bad scan. Okay for the night the elevator broke and he carried her, four flights, like a very tired knight from a very small kingdom. Okay.

· · ·

The arithmetic of slow

A slow disease teaches you a strange kind of arithmetic. Every minute is divisible. Every Sunday is a whole life. The sun setting is a small funeral you attend twice a year, in the spring and in the fall, and it does not feel sad if the right person is sitting next to you on the porch.

They had years, which sounds short to people who get to count in decades. But years are long when you spend them paying attention.

They watched a hundred bad movies. They named seven stray cats and adopted one. They stole grapes from a neighbor’s vine and sang, badly, off-key, the song his father used to whistle at the end of every hard day:

Always look on the bright side of life…

It was a joke at first, the absurdity of it. Two kids with bad lungs and worse odds, whistling a Monty Python song from a film about a man on a cross. But somewhere along the way it stopped being a joke. It became the only theology either of them could believe in. Not optimism. Not denial. Something quieter — like a man on a cross deciding that the sky, at least, was still beautiful.

Bright side, he liked to say, is not what you see when things are good. Bright side is what you decide to see when things refuse to be.

· · ·

Tunnel vision

The thing nobody warns you about a slow death is how attentive it makes you to the wrong things.

By twenty, Mira could tell you the precise weight of August’s hand on hers. She could tell you what his footsteps sounded like on linoleum versus carpet, on Tuesday versus Saturday, in the morning when he was hopeful versus the night when he was pretending to be. She could tell you the smell of the soap on the inside of his wrist, and the exact grade of paleness his face turned when the doctor said we’ll try a different combination.

The world narrowed. Not in a bad way. It narrowed the way a hallway narrows toward a door you are walking toward.

She called it tunnel vision. He laughed and called it a love song.

“You stop seeing the rest of the room,” she said.

“Good,” he said. “The rest of the room was lousy anyway.”

By the time she was twenty-two, the tunnel had shrunk to a single, lit point, and the point was him. She no longer noticed that the curtains were green, or that the IV pole had a small wheel that wobbled, or that the nurse came in every four hours with a clipboard she had stopped pretending to read. She noticed his eyebrows. She noticed the small twitch at the corner of his mouth that meant he was not going to cry until she fell asleep.

Some infinities, she thought, are bigger than other infinities. Some afternoons are bigger than entire lifetimes. She had had so many of those afternoons. More than was fair. More than the long Latin word had promised.

· · ·

Hello from the other side

The night she calls him, the night of hello from the other side, August is not in her room. He is at his sister’s wedding three states away, because Mira had insisted, with the small remaining force of her voice, that he go. I can’t have you missing real things, she’d said. Go dance for me. Come back tomorrow.

He had gone, which is the bravest thing he had ever done.

Now, on the phone, he is whispering from a hallway with bad lighting and a band playing slightly off-key behind a closed door. She can hear, very faintly, a wedding singer butchering an Adele song.

“Are they really,” she says, “playing Hello.”

“They really are.”

“That’s so on-the-nose I might haunt them.”

“Please do.”

They laugh. The laugh costs her something. Most things do, by now.

“Listen,” she says.

She does not say I think this is it. She does not need to. He has known the geometry of this conversation for six years. He has practiced it the way pilots practice landings — carefully, hopefully, knowing it will only ever happen once for real.

Instead, she says: sing with me.

And he does. He stands in a wedding hallway in a borrowed suit, and through a phone with a cracked screen, he sings, very softly, very off-key:

Always look on the bright side of life…

She joins him. Her voice is a thread now, but a thread is enough to sew with.

Always look on the bright side of life…

· · ·

What the chart will not say

The chart, when they file it later, will be very honest and very dull. Cause of death, it will say, in a doctor’s tired handwriting. Respiratory failure secondary to chronic disease.

Death secondary to slow death. The small final thing, standing on the shoulders of the long quiet thing that came before.

But here is what the chart will not say.

It will not say that at the end, she was not afraid.

It will not say that the room had narrowed to one lit point, and that the point was a voice on a phone, in a hallway, in a borrowed suit, three states away.

It will not say that she said okay one last time, in a whisper that he could feel more than hear, and that he, somehow, said okay back.

It will not say that the last thing she heard was a slightly off-key man, three states away, gently insisting to her — and to himself — that there was, despite everything, a bright side. That the tunnel ended in light, not in dark. That hello from the other side was, in the end, just hello.

· · ·

Coda — for anyone reading this from inside a tunnel

If you have ever loved someone who is leaving slowly, you already know the secret of this story.

You already know that slow death is not really death. It is a long, careful goodbye disguised as an illness. It is a tunnel, narrowing, until only the people who matter are left in the frame. It is a song you keep singing past the point where singing makes any sense. It is a phone you keep answering on the third ring, even when you know who it is, and what they are about to say, and what it will cost you to hear it.

You already know that the bright side is not a lie people tell you to feel better.

The bright side is a discipline.

The bright side is a choice you make every morning, with hands that shake a little.

The bright side is a phone, ringing, late at night, from the other side — and someone, somewhere, picking up on the third ring, and saying, before anything else,

Hello.

— for everyone who has loved someone slowly, and well.
